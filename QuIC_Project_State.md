# QuIC Characterization — Project State

This document describes where the project stands, what it is now arguing, and what remains to run. It carries everything except the per-experiment write-ups, which live in the companion experimental-results document. Content here reflects the conversations and decisions to date, including two framing pivots since the project's early stages.

## What the paper is

The object under study is **QuIC**, a fixed, training-free quantum graph embedding introduced in the IEEE QCE 2026 paper (*QuIC: A Training-Free Quantum Graph Embedding with Large-Scale Hardware Validation*, arXiv:2604.18841). One repetition of the circuit applies a uniform rotation layer, one phase per edge, and a uniform mixer, then measures and sorts the Born distribution. The sorted distribution is the representation. This study asks a different question about the same object than the QCE paper did: not whether it runs on hardware, but what structural information it encodes, how that information is organized geometrically, how much of it is classical spectral information in disguise, and whether the non-spectral remainder does functional work.

The target venue is **AAAI 2027**. The contribution is framed as a representation-learning result, not a quantum-algorithms result. The methodology is the graph-learning field's own — linear probes, spectral baselines, GNN comparisons, exhaustive rather than sampled censuses — and the theory is combinatorial rather than quantum-algorithmic. The paper makes no quantum-advantage or efficiency claims anywhere. The cospectral separations that evidence the non-spectral residue are certified real (E2C) and simultaneously certified unmeasurable behind a 10^10 to 10^14 shot wall, and the honesty about that tension is part of the contribution.

Modern graph representation learning is organized around an expressivity question: standard message-passing networks provably cannot count cycles (Chen et al. 2020), and the field's response has been to inject structure — precomputed motif counts (GSN), random node identifiers (RNI), Laplacian positional encodings (LapPE). Every one of those fixes works by handing the network information it cannot compute. QuIC sits at the opposite pole: a circuit with no trained parameters, no injected features, and no structural side channels.

## The object

For a graph $G$ with $n$ vertices and $m$ edges, one repetition of the circuit is

$$U_G = U_M(\beta)\, U_E(G,\gamma)\, U_D(\boldsymbol{\alpha}), \qquad |\psi_G\rangle = U_G|0^n\rangle,$$

where $U_D$ applies $R_X$ rotations (uniform on regular graphs), $U_E$ applies $R_{ZZ}(\gamma)$ on every edge, and $U_M$ applies a uniform $R_X(\beta)$ mixer. The representation is the descending-sorted Born distribution $q_G = \text{sort}_\downarrow \{|\langle y|\psi_G\rangle|^2\}$.

The configuration is locked for the entire study: canonical angles $(\theta_{\mathrm{enc}}, \gamma, \beta) = (2.875, 2.0, 0.1)$, one repetition, exact statevector simulation, full sorted vectors, SEED = 0. The primary datasets are the **complete enumerations** of connected cubic graphs at $n = 14$ (509 graphs) and $n = 16$ (4,060 graphs), generated by nauty. Exhaustive censuses mean cross-validation folds partition a complete population, so the write-ups say "linearly decodable" and never "generalizes."

## Mathematical framework, in brief

Full statements and proofs are in `Mathematical_Framework_QuIC.ipynb` (24 sections). Every identity below is additionally verified numerically inside the experiment notebooks that use it.

**Proposition 1 (cut-phase identity).** The entangler acts diagonally as $U_E|x\rangle = e^{-i\gamma m/2} e^{i\gamma c_G(x)}|x\rangle$, where $c_G(x)$ is the number of edges crossing the cut induced by the bitstring $x$. The circuit therefore encodes the complete graph cut landscape into phases, and QuIC is a fixed hypercube-convolution transform of that landscape.

**Proposition 2 (permutation equivariance).** $q_{\pi G} = q_G$ for every vertex permutation $\pi$. Sorting quotients out the label action, so the representation is a graph-isomorphism invariant.

**Proposition 3 (cut-function completeness).** The labeled cut function determines the adjacency matrix exactly via $A_{ij} = \tfrac{1}{2}\left(c_G(\{i\}) + c_G(\{j\}) - c_G(\{i,j\})\right)$. The signal entering the transform contains the whole graph. This does **not** imply injectivity of the measured, sorted output, and it is not claimed (see "What is not proved" below).

**Theorem 4 (boundary-resolved Fourier expansion).** The Walsh coefficient of the pure cut-phase function on character $T \subseteq V$ is

$$\widehat{f}_G(T) = e^{i\gamma m/2} \cos(\gamma/2)^m \, Z_{G,T}\!\left(-i\tan\tfrac{\gamma}{2}\right), \qquad Z_{G,T}(z) = \sum_{F \subseteq E,\ \partial F = T} z^{|F|},$$

where $\partial F$ is the odd-degree boundary of the edge subset $F$. The transform organizes the graph by boundary sector: the closed sector ($T = \varnothing$) enumerates even subgraphs, and open sectors enumerate $T$-joins. One qualification is stated up front: at the canonical angle, $|\tan(\gamma/2)| \approx 1.557 > 1$, so the expansion orders which structures occur and at what edge order, not their numerical influence. Fixed-angle dominance is established by the experiments, and the small mixer angle ($\tan(\beta/2) \approx 0.05$) supplies the perturbative handle, exposing finite differences of the cut landscape at first order — this is the mechanism E9's β-expansion experiment verifies directly.

**Corollary 5 (cubic closed-sector coefficients).** On cubic graphs, even subgraphs are vertex-disjoint cycle unions, so $[z^3]\, Z_{G,\varnothing} = C_3$, $[z^4]\, Z_{G,\varnothing} = C_4$, $[z^5]\, Z_{G,\varnothing} = C_5$, and $[z^6]\, Z_{G,\varnothing} = C_6 + \binom{C_3}{2} - D$, where $D$ counts diamonds ($K_4$ minus an edge). The theory hands the experiments the exact hierarchy $C_3 \to C_4 \to C_5$ that E1 then observes geometrically.

**Corollary 6 (diamond sign split).** On cubic graphs the sixth spectral moment satisfies $\text{tr}(A^6) = 87n + 6C_3 + 96C_4 + 12(C_6 + D)$, so the spectrum pins $S_6 = C_6 + D$, while the sixth closed cut sector carries $Q_6 = C_6 - D$. The two representations expose complementary signed combinations, and jointly they pin $C_6$ and $D$ individually. This is the sharpest exact distinction between QuIC and the spectrum. It is also, as the review correction below explains, weaker evidence for a non-spectral residue than it first appears — C6 and D tie together within every cospectral group, so this identity alone does not separate the two representations there.

**Corollary 7 (distance valuation).** The lowest-degree term of the pair-boundary polynomial satisfies $\text{val}\, Z_{G,\{u,v\}} = d_G(u,v)$, with leading coefficient equal to the number of shortest paths. The boundary-resolved transform structurally contains the distance matrix, hence the Wiener index and radius. This is the theoretical explanation for exactly the invariants the cospectral audit (E8A) found in the non-spectral residue.

**Proposition 8 (constant-feature MPNN collapse).** Any permutation-invariant message-passing network with uniform initial features produces the identical embedding for every graph in a fixed-$(n, d)$ regular census. This is the theorem behind the E3 constant-feature rows, where the collapse is also measured rather than assumed.

**Supporting spectral identities on cubic graphs** (all verified exactly on both censuses): $C_3 = \text{tr}(A^3)/6$, $\ C_4 = (\text{tr}(A^4) - 15n)/8$, $\ C_5 = (\text{tr}(A^5) - 10\text{tr}(A^3))/10$, and girth is a threshold function of $C_3$ through $C_5$ at these sizes. The entire cycle table is spectral information on cubic graphs, which is what makes the residue question sharp. Off regularity, the general identities $\text{tr}(A^3) = 6C_3$ and $\text{tr}(A^4) = 8C_4 + 2\sum d_i^2 - \sum d_i$ survive, while the $C_5$ identity breaks. E6 and E6D are designed around exactly that breakage schedule.

**What is not proved.** Injectivity of the measured, sorted representation is not claimed. The Born map erases coordinate-dependent phases and sorting erases output permutations, so measured injectivity would require a uniform anti-aliasing theorem that does not exist yet. Choosing $\pi$-irrational angles prevents periodic aliasing of distinct integer cut values and nothing more.

## Why the framing changed

The project has gone through two pivots since its early stages.

### First pivot: applications to characterization

The original framing was an applications story. That framing was killed by the n = 12 PCA — the embedding did not cleanly support the downstream-task result the applications angle needed — and the paper became a theory-heavy characterization instead: what does QuIC encode, how is that information organized, and how much of it is spectral versus non-spectral.

### The reviewer risk that forced the second pivot

A characterization paper is publishable at AAAI without a conventional application, but only if it answers a broader AI question than "what does this particular circuit encode?" AAAI regularly publishes expressivity and formal-characterization work — the Weisfeiler–Leman GNN-expressivity line is the reference class — but those papers characterize a broad model class, and QuIC as written to this point is one fixed circuit at one depth and one parameter setting.

That creates the central reviewer question: **why should the AI community care what this one handcrafted quantum feature map happens to encode?** The honest assessment is that the characterization alone is borderline rather than a clear accept. A graph-representation reviewer might find the theory–experiment correspondence sufficient; a conventional empirical reviewer will ask where the representation improves an actual learning problem; a quantum-ML reviewer will ask why the exponentially large probability vector is useful; a skeptical reviewer will classify the work as analysis of a single fixed circuit rather than an AI contribution.

### The resolution: sorting is the contribution

The strongest available reframing moves the object of study from QuIC to the **sorted readout itself**. Sorting the measurement distribution is a generic invariant readout that converts any label-equivariant quantum graph circuit into a graph representation: vertex relabeling permutes computational-basis probabilities, and sorting quotients out that action. Under this framing the contribution is no longer "QuIC happens to know triangles, cycles, and some metric structure." It becomes:

> Quantum graph circuits generate label-equivariant probability landscapes. Sorting provides a general invariant quotient, and circuit dynamics determine a tunable hierarchy of graph information within that quotient.

That claim is broad enough to be an AI contribution rather than a single-circuit analysis. Establishing it requires two things the current results do not yet supply: a demonstration that the discovered structure survives across more than one circuit and more than one parameter setting, and a clean measurement of how much information sorting discards relative to an information-preserving invariant labeling and how much it retains relative to coarse symmetric summaries. Both are now scoped as experiments (Wave 3, below).

### A correction that reshaped the residue argument

An external review corrected a load-bearing claim: the E2R diamonds row cannot by itself prove that QuIC carries non-spectral information, because C6 and D tie within every cospectral group (the tr(A^6) identity pins C6 + D). E2R shows a gain over a *linear spectral readout*, not over the spectrum itself. The genuinely non-spectral evidence is the functional cospectral challenge (E8B), where the spectrum ties by construction. This correction is why the finite-shot experiment, a stronger classical baseline, and the exact-cospectral functional test all rose in priority, and it is reflected above in Corollary 6's discussion.

## Summary of experimental proof

**Proved.** The QuIC entangler encodes the complete graph cut function into computational-basis phases, and the whole circuit is a fixed, permutation-invariant hypercube transform of that cut landscape (Propositions 1 and 2). The labeled cut function determines the adjacency matrix exactly, so the transform's input contains the whole graph (Proposition 3; injectivity of the measured, sorted output is explicitly not claimed). The transform's Walsh coefficients factor by boundary sector into generating polynomials of edge subsets (Theorem 4). On cubic graphs the closed sector's first coefficients are exactly $C_3$, $C_4$, $C_5$, and its sixth coefficient carries $C_6 - D$ while the adjacency spectrum carries $C_6 + D$ (Corollaries 5 and 6). Pair-boundary sectors carry graph distances, hence the Wiener index and radius, in their lowest-degree terms (Corollary 7). Every constant-feature message-passing network provably collapses to a single point on a fixed-degree regular census (Proposition 8). Supporting exact identities verified on both full censuses: $C_3$, $C_4$, $C_5$, and girth are spectral invariants on cubic graphs, and $\text{tr}(A^6) = 87n + 6C_3 + 96C_4 + 12(C_6 + D)$.

**Measured, on complete enumerations (509 + 4,060 cubic graphs).** The embedding geometry realizes the proved hierarchy: triangles globally, $C_4$ within triangle strata, $C_5$ within joint strata. A ridge probe on the untrained sorted vector linearly decodes $C_3$ and $C_4$ at 1.000, $C_5$ at 0.98, and girth at 0.999 at n = 16, and the strongest trained GNN arm (GIN + LapPE) loses every cycle row to it while the constant-feature arms collapse with spread exactly zero across 210 models, as Proposition 8 requires. There is no nonlinear headroom anywhere: the linear table is the representation's ceiling. All 46 cospectral pairs across both censuses separate, certified real at 2.5e+07 to 2.9e+10 times the measured float64 rounding floor. The residue's functional carriers match Corollary 7's prediction: within cospectral groups, only Wiener index, radius, and automorphism order vary.

**Resolved since the earlier draft of this document.** The diamond sign split (Corollary 6) has its direct empirical test landed: E2R's diamonds row shows ΔR² of +0.34 (n = 14) and +0.31 (n = 16) — functionally confirmed as a residual-space result, with the caveat above that this is a gain over a linear spectral readout, not proof of information absent from the spectrum entirely. E6 and E6D both ran: QuIC does not beat the spectral baseline on C5 off regularity in either the flat or the degree-encoded arm, closing that question as a measured negative on two arms (E6R, a two-repetition follow-up, is in flight). E8B ran and found functional non-spectral signal — 14/14 on automorphism order at k = 100, 18/26 on Wiener index — but its own permutation audit found the empirical null heavier than the binomial assumption, so the confirmation experiment (E12) with full-refit permutation tests is required before this result is final.

## Where the evidence stands now

### Solid and complete

The core empirical spine is complete on both censuses: the stratified hierarchy (E1, n = 14; n = 16 replication in flight after a Mantel-statistic rewrite), linear decodability (E2), the moment ladder, the spectral decomposition (E2.5), the GNN comparison (E3), the folklore 2-WL baseline (E3C), the nonlinear-readout negatives (E5), truncation curves (E7), the exact cospectral audit (E8A), and the precision audit (E2C). The spectral-baseline (E2S) and residual-probe (E2R) experiments have landed at n = 14, with E2R timing out at n = 16 after the diamonds target — this is what E2R-c exists to finish. E8B, the functional cospectral challenge, has landed with the permutation-audit caveat above.

### The results that reshaped the immediate queue

Two recent outcomes changed the immediate work.

First, the E2R n = 16 run timed out after diamonds, so radius, Wiener index, and log2|Aut| are missing at n = 16 and need a targeted completion run (E2R-c).

Second, E8B's own permutation audit found the empirical null tail heavier than the binomial (95th percentile 19 versus 17), because the leave-one-group-out models share overlapping training sets and produce correlated correctness indicators. The nominal binomial p-values are therefore not exact for this pipeline. Final inference on both the 14/14 automorphism result and the 18/26 Wiener result requires full-refit permutation tests, which is what E12 supplies.

### Off-regularity scope, now closed on two encoder arms

The decodability results are properties of QuIC on regular censuses. E6 (flat encoder) confirmed that QuIC does not beat the spectral baseline on C5 off regularity, where the C5 identity provably breaks. E6D (degree encoder) confirmed that adding per-vertex degree encoding does not restore off-regularity decodability. Both encoder arms are measured negatives at one repetition, and E6R tests whether a second repetition changes that. The scope claim — decodability is a regular-census property, with the framework's Section 20 supplying the mechanism — now stands on multiple measured arms rather than one.

## The generalization program

The A/B/C experiment package, together with eleven scheduling rulings, defines the work that both completes the current characterization and begins the extension to the sorted-readout story. Experiment IDs continue the E-series (this is the EN naming, the correct one going forward). Scheduling sequence is set by Luke; the build order below reflects the approved waves, not a fixed calendar.

### Wave 1 — completion (vectors already exist, buildable immediately)

These defend the headline results and reuse existing probability vectors.

- **E2R-c** completes the timed-out E2R n = 16 arm for radius, Wiener index, and log2|Aut| only, with the diamonds row re-run as a reproduction gate. *(Built and delivered; the n = 14 dry run reproduced all four recorded E2R rows exactly.)*
- **E10** (was A1) validates the high-dimensional probes: solver-agreement arms (primal, SVD, dual, plus a conservative regularization grid) on C3, C5, C6, and diamonds across both censuses, with a shuffled-target null control. It absorbs the old E0. *(Built and delivered. The n = 14 dry run passed the three primary criteria — the null stays at chance, every observed score beats every shuffled run, and the solvers agree. The conservative-grid arm fails informatively: the informative small-β signal lives in a subspace below the regularization floor, so the near-interpolation regularization is required by signal scale and is certified safe by the passing null. The paper sentence is fixed accordingly — "safety certified by a shuffled-target null at the same alphas," not "robust to regularization choice.")*
- **E11** (was A2) tests nonlinear spectral adequacy: linear, degree-2 polynomial, RBF-kernel, and ExtraTrees spectral first stages against QuIC on cross-fitted residuals, targeting diamonds, C6, girth, and a C3 control. The headline question is whether the diamonds gain of +0.31 to +0.34 survives nonlinear spectral baselines.
- **E12** (was A4) confirms E8B with full-refit permutation tests, leaving the E8B run untouched. It uses k = 100 as the pre-registered primary depth, exhaustively enumerates all 2^14 group-wise sign flips for the automorphism result, adds a sampled max-over-k selection correction, runs 50,000 Wiener permutations, and evaluates the classical baselines (cumulative 2-WL, stable 2-WL, shortest-path histogram, spectrum) through the identical group-preserving ranking.
- **E13** (was A5) is the truncation decomposition: raw head, normalized head, mass-only, and normalized-plus-mass representations across truncation depths, completing the cospectral tracer the E7 write-up flags as unfinished. It separates whether the predictive content lives in the head's shape, its retained mass, or both.

### Wave 2 — high-value extension

- **E14** (was A3) is the n = 18 selective cospectral audit. It enumerates the 41,301-graph cubic census with cheap classical invariants only, groups exactly by trace tuples, and computes QuIC vectors solely for groups that vary in diamonds, C6, Wiener index, or automorphism order, plus matched invariant-identical controls. A single diamond-varying cospectral group is the direct Corollary 6 witness.
- **E15** (was A6) is the finite-shot recovery experiment, extending E7S across sampling budgets that span the review's range and the measured emergence region. The primary protocol trains on noisy sorted vectors and tests on independently sampled noisy vectors, and reports per-target minimum shot counts to reach 90% and 95% of exact-vector performance.

### Wave 3 — generalization screen (likely after the abstract deadline)

This wave is where the sorted-readout story is actually tested.

- **E16 / E17** (B1 / B2) are the readout-quotient and label-dependence ablations over the complete n = 8 census and the n = 14 cubic census, comparing arbitrary, canonical, and independently randomized labelings against the sorted readout. They produce the quotient table that quantifies how much sorting discards relative to an information-preserving canonical labeling and how much it retains relative to probability moments.
- **E18** (C1) is the six-circuit canonical screen — a preparation-only control, a phase-only control, canonical QuIC, a QAOA-preparation variant, a two-repetition variant, and an alternative-mixer variant — producing a circuit-by-target matrix. The two controls are hard-asserted graph-blind, and the two-repetition circuit is built from the E6R reference implementation. This is the experiment that determines which structural signals are QuIC-specific, which are architecture-level, and which are readout-level.
- **E19** (C2) is the angle-response map, roughly 24 configurations per circuit, testing whether the observed structure is generic over parameter space or concentrated near the canonical point.
- **E20** (C3) is the n = 8 partition audit, comparing the equivalence classes each sorted readout induces against spectrum, 1-WL, 2-WL, the short-cycle tuple, and the canonical raw distribution. Collisions are certified through the sanctioned independent-implementation and extended-precision machinery, used for collision certification only.
- **E21** (C4) is the regularity-transfer experiment across cubic, the complete n = 12 4-regular census, and nonregular graphs, using the three strongest circuits from E18.
- **E22** (C5) measures circuit-information similarity and complementarity, with concatenation tests last.

The generalized story becomes supportable when the evidence shows that an equivariant quantum circuit plus a sorted probability readout reliably produces a nontrivial graph invariant across more than one circuit and more than one parameter configuration, while the circuit architecture predictably controls which graph information is retained.

## Standing rules and constraints in force

- Package C screens use k = 1000 features throughout; full vectors are reserved for survivors.
- Circuits in the generalization screen are named QP, QE, QuIC-1, QAOA-1, QuIC-2, QuIC-Y.
- Storage: full float64 vectors are kept only for canonical configurations per census and circuit; every angle-map and screen point keeps the top-1000 head, the retained mass, and a SHA-256 fingerprint of the full sorted vector, with collision screening done in-session at full precision before discard. The n = 8 census keeps everything; n = 18 keeps only qualifying-group vectors. Projected footprint is 2 to 3 GB rather than 10.
- Experiment costs are scoped for reasonable defense, not exhaustiveness.
- Methodology discipline after the E2C audit: the established qiskit simulation pipeline is used verbatim, methodology substitutions require sign-off before use, and code is written to be auditable — descriptive names, commented methods, comprehensible structure.
- Writing begins on schedule regardless of experiment state. The core paper already exists in the completed spine; the generalization screen strengthens the framing but is not a prerequisite for a submission.

## Provenance and reproducibility

Every dataset is a complete enumeration or a seeded, deduplicated sample. SEED = 0 end to end. The circuit configuration is locked and appears identically in every producer. Load-bearing identities carry in-notebook exact asserts on the full censuses. The cross-validation splits are pickled once and asserted everywhere they are reused.
